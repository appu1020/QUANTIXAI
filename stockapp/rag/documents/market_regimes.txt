Market Regimes and Trading Strategy Knowledge
============================================

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
MARKET REGIMES
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

BULL MARKET
A bull market is defined as a sustained rise of 20% or more from recent lows.

Characteristics:
- Rising prices across most assets.
- Strong economic fundamentals (GDP growth, low unemployment).
- Investor optimism and high risk appetite.
- Increasing earnings per share (EPS).
- Low credit spreads.

Trading Approach:
- Trend-following strategies perform well.
- Buy pullbacks to moving averages (SMA50, EMA20).
- Long bias — breakouts above resistance have higher success rates.
- Sector rotation: early bull favors financials, tech; late bull favors energy, materials.

BEAR MARKET
A bear market is defined as a sustained decline of 20% or more from recent highs.

Characteristics:
- Declining prices, often over months to years.
- Weak economic fundamentals (recession fears, rising unemployment).
- Investor pessimism and low risk appetite.
- Declining corporate earnings.
- Widening credit spreads.

Trading Approach:
- Shorting (selling) strategies are applicable.
- Sell rallies to key resistance levels.
- Defensive sectors outperform: utilities, healthcare, consumer staples.
- Cash preservation is a valid strategy.
- Short duration bonds and gold often perform well.

RANGING / SIDEWAYS MARKET
A market where price oscillates between identifiable support and resistance
without a clear directional trend.

Characteristics:
- ADX below 20 — weak trend.
- Price bouncing between established levels.
- Indicators like RSI and Stochastic RSI are most useful.
- Breakouts fail frequently (false breakouts).

Trading Approach:
- Buy near support, sell near resistance (mean-reversion strategies).
- Oscillators (RSI, Stochastic) provide better signals than moving averages.
- Use tight stop losses — swings are limited.
- Avoid trend-following strategies; they generate whipsaws.

HIGH VOLATILITY REGIME
Characterized by rapid, large price swings in either direction.

Characteristics:
- VIX index > 30 (fear index elevated).
- ATR significantly above its historical average.
- Wide bid-ask spreads.
- News-driven price action dominates technical signals.

Trading Approach:
- Reduce position sizes (ATR-based sizing).
- Widen stop losses to account for noise.
- Avoid overtrading — volatility generates many false signals.
- Options strategies benefit from high implied volatility (selling premium).
- Trend reversals are frequent and fast.

LOW VOLATILITY REGIME
Characterized by small, slow price movements.

Characteristics:
- VIX index below 15.
- ATR at or below historical average.
- Markets trending slowly upward (typical of bull market mid-phase).
- Complacency can precede sudden volatility spikes.

Trading Approach:
- Momentum strategies work well.
- Breakouts from tight consolidation are more reliable.
- Carry trades and income strategies are favored.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
RISK MANAGEMENT PRINCIPLES
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

STOP LOSS
A stop loss order automatically exits a position when price reaches a
predetermined adverse level, limiting maximum loss.

Best Practices:
- ATR-based stop: Set stop at 1.5-2x ATR below entry for longs.
- Structure-based stop: Place stop below key support (for longs).
- Never move stop further away from entry to avoid losses.
- Typical stop: 1-3% of trade value for swing trades.

TAKE PROFIT / TARGET LEVELS
Risk-to-reward ratio should be at least 1:2 (risk $1 to make $2).

Common Target Techniques:
- Next resistance level for long trades.
- Fibonacci extension levels (1.272x, 1.618x move).
- Fixed R-multiple targets (e.g., 2R, 3R where R = stop distance).

POSITION SIZING
Position sizing determines how much capital to risk per trade.

Kelly Criterion (simplified): Position Size = Win Rate - (Loss Rate / Win-Loss Ratio)
Conservative: Risk 1-2% of total capital per trade.
Aggressive: Risk up to 5% of total capital per trade.

SHARPE RATIO
Sharpe Ratio = (Strategy Return - Risk-Free Rate) / Standard Deviation of Returns

Interpretation:
- < 0: Strategy underperforms risk-free rate.
- 0–1: Adequate returns for risk taken.
- 1–2: Good risk-adjusted performance.
- > 2: Excellent performance (rare in live trading).

MAXIMUM DRAWDOWN
The maximum observed loss from a peak to a trough before a new peak.

Interpretation:
- < 10%: Conservative, appropriate for institutional capital.
- 10-20%: Moderate — acceptable for most retail strategies.
- > 30%: High drawdown — difficult psychologically to sustain.
- Recovery time from drawdown can take months to years.

WIN RATE AND PROFIT FACTOR
Win Rate = Winning Trades / Total Trades
Profit Factor = Gross Profits / Gross Losses

A strategy can be profitable with a low win rate if the average win is much
larger than the average loss (high risk-reward ratio).

Example: 40% win rate with 3:1 reward-to-risk = profitable strategy.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
SECTOR ROTATION
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Different economic phases favor different sectors:

Early Recession: Consumer Staples, Healthcare, Utilities (defensive)
Late Recession / Recovery: Financials, Technology (growth)
Early Bull / Expansion: Technology, Consumer Discretionary, Industrials
Late Bull: Energy, Materials, Real Estate (cyclical)

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
COMMON TRADING MISTAKES
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

1. Overtrading: Taking too many positions leads to excessive commissions and emotional decisions.
2. Moving Stop Losses: Letting losses run beyond planned exit destroys accounts.
3. Revenge Trading: Trading emotionally after losses to "make it back" quickly.
4. Ignoring Risk-Reward: Taking trades with poor reward-to-risk ratios.
5. Confirmation Bias: Only looking for signals that confirm existing opinion.
6. Overconfidence: Past success does not guarantee future results.
7. No Trading Plan: Entering trades without defined entry, exit, and risk rules.
8. Ignoring Market Context: Technical signals work differently in different regimes.
